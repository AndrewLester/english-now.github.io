Well-Being

The first and highest priority for all of us during the coronavirus crisis is the physical health and well-being of ourselves and our loved ones. Please take advantage of the following efforts to attend the well-being of the community.

  • First, consider joining a small group discussion led by Jane Turner on Wednesdays, Spirit Chat with Friends. Jane’s hope for these conversations is that we inform, support and inspire each other, allowing each participant adequate time (up to three to five minutes) per sharing opportunity, without interruption. This group is intended to be a place to get together personally – not as a place to talk about work (though that may come up) – and as such the group will honor the privacy of participants. And if this group grows too large, as appears to be happening, we’ll add more of these groups. Email info@english-now.com if you’d like to join Jane’s or another group like this.
  • Next, a great resource is available to our team in the form of an online class called “The Science of Well-Being.” This is a class developed by Yale University Professor Laurie Santos, delivered on the online platform Coursera. This is a fantastic class and was even a subject of a BBC article, A college course (the) world wants to study right now. Over 2.4 million people are enrolled in the class worldwide (!) as of May 15. For more information on this class, see here and watch for a Paperless Post invitation to Zoom Webinars that introduce how the Coursera platform work, introduce this class, and invite discussion about how this and other Coursera classes may be meaningful to our school community.
  • Finally – to be very direct – there is no question that the technology we’re all engaging at these times is a major source of stress. And we’re denied the very human, side-by-side interaction, that reassures us all, as one clicks through new screens and tries new things online. So, as we confront “edtech trials” please feel free to raise your hand if you need help. Please tell Jennifer or email info@english-now.com about specific training requests.

Thank you all for your flexibility, adaptability, and for caring for each other well. Please keep an eye on your own and each other’s well-being – and consider this topic a priority for our community in the coming months.

Online Curriculum Resources for Adults (watch this space, this list will grow)

  1. News for You: This is the same resource that we have been using in the classroom, now available - and fully-functional - online. Go to the News for You website: newreaderspress.com – Hover your mouse over the "News For You" tab on the right side, then click on News For You Home. In the white window, type in 22667F.
  2. Breaking News English: Current events stories - includes mini-lessons with answers and audio available in five speeds.
  3. Englishpage.com: Grammar review and exercises on many topics, great for students to reference independently or use in class
  4. Readworks: a free resource for K-12 educators with readings searchable by grade/Lexile level/topic; used in MCPS
  5. Voice of America: many short news stories, kept current, with the ability to change the pace of playback sped
  6. National Public Radio: all your favorite programs, and many stories have transcripts that students can review as prework or together
  7. TED: TED Talks are a great resource generally, highlighted in National Geographic curriculum including Keynote, and available online with transcripts in English and often dozens of other languages--assigning TED Talks as prework then playing a segment (or reviewing a segment of the transcript, if audio is an issue) can be a great way to spark conversation. See the PDF presentation from a professional development session, “Teaching with TED Talks”, led by Jennifer Kagan. In addition, download and use a great, one-page rubric that supports students' critical thinking about TED Talks. Email this rubric to students - and use it in class.
  8. Easy English News. This is the same resource that we have been using in the classroom, now available as a downloadable PDF online. Go to the Easy English News website.
  9. Words to Use: Developed by a copywriter, Amy Morse, Words to Use is "a new kind of word reference that can help you write about anything! Unlike a thesaurus, which groups words by their meaning, we group subject-related words by parts of speech." Try sending a specific page to students when you want them to write or speak about a particular subject, such as movies and TV.
  10. Breaking News English is an English Now! favorite for discussing the news with students. It includes English News Lessons in 7 levels with a multi-level listening, scroll-reading activities, and all-skills activities. See the PDF presentation from or the recording of a great professional development session led by Jennifer Kagan.
  11. Vocabulary development resources include: Dictionary.com, useful for looking up words, hearing their pronunciation, and review of sample sentences; and thesaurus.com, for synonyms and antonyms--use the writing tool to mouse over words you would like to replace and find other options. See the PDF presentation from or the recording of another great professional development session led by Jennifer Kagan.
  12. Storycorps is an excellent resource for practicing listening skills and teaching the art of storytelling with adults or high-school students. Through the Storycorps web site, students can access many interviews on a variety of themes with accompanying transcripts. Teacher resources are available for developing lessons based on Storycorps interviews. This recording highlights some of the ways to use the Storycorps web site and support materials with your students. Listen to the recording of a professional development session, featuring a great contribution by English Now! colleague Barbara Lewis.
  13. News for You is a great weekly publication on paper and is making online access available during the COVID-19 crisis. For access, go to the News for You publisher's website: www.newreaderspress.com, hover your mouse over News For You tab on the right side, then click on News For You Home. In the white window, type in 22667F. And see the PDF presentation from or the recording of a professional development session led by Jennifer Kagan about how to use News for You online.

**NEW** Online Curriculum Resources for Youth/Teens (watch this space, this list will grow too)

  1. Scholastic Learn at Home: Access 20 days of learning journeys and three hours of grade level-specific learning opportunities per day. Includes projects based on articles and stories, virtual field trips, reading and geography challenges.
  2. TED Ed: High-quality, interactive, video-based lessons for free. Lesson plans are organized by age groups, spanning all subjects. Also, you can can subscribe to lesson plans delivered every day by email.
  3. Time for Kids: Age-appropriate stories that kids are excited about. Information just for kids that explains the world around them. And check out the Free Digital Library Available Through the Year-End, linked at the top of the page.
  4. Smithsonian's History Explorer: Teachers can find interactive lessons and show their students museum artifacts. Provides online resources for teaching K-12 students about American history.
  5. PBS Learning Media: Resources for PreK-12 students. Materials include lesson plans, videos, news articles, and activities. Also, includes a professional development section.
  6. National Geographic: Maps, imagery, and a resource library are all available for free. Lots of lesson plans, articles, and interactive lessons for K-12 students.
